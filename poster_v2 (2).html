Testing Quantum Hypotheses — Entanglement Detection

RATHINDRA NATH KARMAKAR • SAMRIDDHA LAHIRY • KOICHI YAMAGATA

Classical
Models & Simple Tests
Model: A probability measure \(P\) on a measurable space \((\Omega, \mathcal{F})\). We observe a random variable \(X\).
Simple Test: To decide \(H_0: P_0\) vs \(H_1: P_1\), a test is a measurable function \(\phi: \Omega \to [0,1]\).
Errors:
\[\alpha(\phi) = \E_{P_0}[\phi(X)]\]
\[\beta(\phi) = \E_{P_1}[1-\phi(X)]\]
Gaussian Distributions (The Limit Objects)
Gaussian \(\mathcal{N}_2(\mu, V)\): A random vector \(X \in \mathbb{R}^2\) completely characterized by its characteristic function:
\[\E[\exp(i u^\top X)] = \exp\big(i u^\top \mu - \frac{1}{2} u^\top V u\big)\]
where \(V\) is a positive definite covariance matrix. All classical variables commute.
Local Testing & Likelihood Expansion
Local Problem: Given \(n\) i.i.d. samples from \(P_\theta\), test \(H_0: \theta = \theta_0\) vs \(H_1: \theta_{n,h} = \theta_0 + h/\sqrt{n}\).
Test statistic: We can work with either the log-likelihood ratio or expand it to get the score statistic \(Z_n\):
\[\log \prod \frac{p_{\theta_{n,h}}}{p_{\theta_0}} = h^\top Z_n - \frac{1}{2} h^\top I_{\theta_0} h + o_P(1)\]
where \(I_{\theta_0}\) is the Fisher Information matrix.
Convergence & Asymptotic Representation
Convergence: By the CLT, the score converges in distribution under \(H_0\):
\[Z_n \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, I_{\theta_0})\]
Representation (Le Cam): Consider any sequence of statistics \(T^{(n)}\). If, for each fixed \(h\), the law of \(T^{(n)}\) under \(P_{\theta_0+h/\sqrt n}^{\otimes n}\) converges, then there exists a (possibly randomized) statistic \(T^{(\infty)}\) on the Gaussian shift experiment
\[\Bigl\{ \mathcal{N}(I_{\theta_0}h,\; I_{\theta_0}) : h \in \R^d \Bigr\}\]
such that
\[\Law_{P^{\otimes(n)}_{\theta_0+h/\sqrt n}}\!\bigl(T^{(n)}\bigr) \Longrightarrow \Law_{\mathcal{N}(I_{\theta_0}h,\; I_{\theta_0})}(T^{(\infty)})\]
So any asymptotic testing or estimation procedure for the local i.i.d. model has a limit procedure on the Gaussian shift model.
Quantum
Models & Simple Tests
Model: A complex Hilbert space \(\mathcal{H}\). A state is a density operator \(\rho\) (self-adjoint, \(\rho \ge 0, \Tr(\rho)=1\)).
Simple Test: To decide \(H_0: \rho_0\) vs \(H_1: \rho_1\), a test dictates a measurement choice via an operator \(T \in \Lop(\Hsp)\) where \(0 \le T \le I\).
Errors:
\[\alpha(T) = \Tr(\rho_0 T)\]
\[\beta(T) = \Tr(\rho_1(I-T))\]
Gaussian Distributions (The Limit Objects)
Quantum Gaussian \(\mathcal{N}_2(\mu, \Sigma)\): A pair \((\rho, R)\), where \(\rho\) operates on canonical observables \(R = (Q, P)^\top\) that strictly do not commute: \([Q, P] = 2i\).
Characterized by the non-commutative analogue of the characteristic function:
\[\Tr\big(\rho \exp(i u^\top R)\big) = \exp\big(i u^\top \mu - \frac{1}{2} u^\top V u\big)\]
Covariance: \(\Sigma = V + \frac{i}{2}S \ge 0\), where \(S\) encodes the canonical commutation relation.
Local Testing & Likelihood Expansion
Local Problem: Given \(n\) copies of a state \(\rho_\theta\), test \(H_0: \theta = \theta_0\) vs \(H_1: \theta_{n,h} = \theta_0 + h/\sqrt{n}\).
Quantum statistic: In q-LAN the relevant expansion uses the quantum log-likelihood ratio \(\mathcal{R}_h^{(n)}\):
\[\log \big(\mathcal{R}_h^{(n)}\big)^2 = h^\top \Delta^{(n)} - \frac{1}{2} h^\top J_{\theta_0} h + o_D(1)\]
where \(\Delta^{(n)}\) are Symmetric Logarithmic Derivatives and \(J_{\theta_0}\) is the Quantum Fisher Info.
Convergence & Asymptotic Representation
Convergence: The SLD observables converge in distribution to a Quantum Gaussian state:
\[\big(\rho_{\theta_0}^{\otimes n},\Delta^{(n)}\big) \rightsquigarrow \mathcal{N}_2(0, J_{\theta_0})\]
Representation: Consider any sequence of measurements \(T^{(n)}\). If, for each fixed \(h\), the corresponding outcome law converges weakly, then there exists a measurement \(T^{(\infty)}\) on the limiting quantum Gaussian shift experiment
\[\Bigl\{ \mathcal{N}\bigl((\Ree \tau)h,\; \Sigma\bigr) : h \in \R^d \Bigr\}\]
such that
\[\Law_{\rho_{\theta_0+h/\sqrt{n}}^{\otimes n}}\!\bigl(T^{(n)}\bigr) \Longrightarrow \Law_{\phi_h}(T^{(\infty)})\]
where \(\phi_h \sim \mathcal{N}((\Ree \tau)h,\Sigma)\) is the limiting quantum Gaussian state. So any asymptotic measurement procedure for the local i.i.d. quantum model has a limit procedure on the quantum Gaussian shift model.
The Separability Hypothesis
The Separability Hypothesis (Projective Norm)

Let \(\rho_\theta \in \cS(\mathcal{H}_A \otimes \mathcal{H}_B)\) be a parameterized bipartite quantum state. Entanglement is geometrically quantified by the projective tensor norm:

\[\|\rho_\theta\|_\pi = \inf \left\{ \sum_i \|A_i\|_1 \|B_i\|_1 \;\middle|\; \rho_\theta = \sum_i A_i \otimes B_i \right\}\]

For a normalized state, it is separable if and only if \(\|\rho_\theta\|_\pi = 1\). The test for entanglement is the non-linear boundary test:

\[H_0: \|\rho_\theta\|_\pi^2 - 1 \le 0 \quad \text{(Separable)} \quad \text{vs} \quad H_1: \|\rho_\theta\|_\pi^2 - 1 > 0 \quad \text{(Entangled)}\]
Unique Dual Witness
Differentiating the Boundary

Fix a separable boundary state at parameter \(\theta_0\), where \(\|\rho_{\theta_0}\|_\pi = 1\). Let \(W_{\theta_0}\) be a dual witness satisfying \(\|W_{\theta_0}\|_\epsilon = 1\) and \(\Ree \Tr(W_{\theta_0} \rho_{\theta_0}) = 1\).

If \(W_{\theta_0}\) is unique, the squared norm \(f(\theta) = \|\rho_\theta\|_\pi^2\) is Fréchet differentiable at \(\theta_0\). The gradient \(c = \nabla f(\theta_0)\) is the vector:

\[c = 2 \left( \Ree \Tr \left( W_{\theta_0} \frac{\partial \rho_\theta}{\partial \theta_j}\Big|_{\theta_0} \right) \right)_{j=1}^2\]

This successfully linearizes the geometric boundary of separable states.

The Limiting Half-Space Test
The Limiting Half-Space Test

By the qLAN Representation Theorem, the i.i.d. sequence of local experiments \(\{\rho_{\theta_0 + h/\sqrt{n}}^{\otimes n}\}\) converges to the limiting Quantum Gaussian Shift Experiment

\[\cE_\infty = \{ \rho_{\theta_h,\Sigma} \mid h \in \mathbb{R}^2 \},\qquad \theta_h = (\Ree \tau) h,\]

represented on canonical observables

\[R = (Q,P)^\top .\]

Under this \(1/\sqrt{n}\) zooming, the non-linear separability constraint locally flattens. In the Gaussian limit experiment, the induced testing problem becomes the half-space test

\[H_0: c^\top \theta_h \le 0 \quad \text{vs} \quad H_1: c^\top \theta_h > 0,\]

where \(\theta_h = (\Ree\tau)h\) is the mean parameter of the limiting Gaussian shift model.

Nuisance Elimination
Nuisance Parameter Elimination

We orthogonally decompose the mean parameter \(\theta_h\) of the Gaussian shift:

\[\theta_h = \theta_\eta \frac{c}{\|c\|^2} + \theta_\xi \frac{d}{\|d\|^2},\qquad d=\begin{pmatrix}-c_2\ c_1\end{pmatrix},\qquad c^\top d=0,\]

where

\[\theta_\eta = c^\top \theta_h,\qquad \theta_\xi = d^\top \theta_h.\]

Thus \(\theta_\eta\) is the parameter of interest and \(\theta_\xi\) is the nuisance parameter.

Translations along the nuisance direction are implemented by

\[V_t = D_{td} = \exp\!\left(i\frac{t}{2} c^\top R\right),\]

and satisfy

\[V_t \rho_{\theta_h,\Sigma} V_t^* = \rho_{\theta_h + td,\Sigma}.\]

By the Quantum Hunt-Stein Theorem, minimizing worst-case error forces the optimal test \(T\) to be invariant under \(V_t\), i.e., \(V_tTV_t^*=T\quad (\forall t\in\mathbb R).\) Thus, by the spectral theorem, every invariant test is of the form

\[T=f(A_c),\qquad A_c=c^\top R,\]

for some bounded Borel function \(f:\R\to[0,1]\).

Optimal Half-Space Test
Final Form of the Optimal Test

Because \(A_c = c^\top R = c_1Q + c_2P\) is a single operator, measuring it yields a classical random variable. Under the Gaussian limit state \(\rho_{\theta_h,\Sigma}\), the outcome is distributed as

\[A_c \sim \mathcal{N}(c^\top \theta_h,\; c^\top V c),\qquad V=\Ree(\Sigma).\]
  • The Limiting Test: Let \(\sigma = \sqrt{c^\top V c}\) and \(z_\alpha = \Phi^{-1}(1-\alpha)\). The Uniformly Most Powerful invariant level-\(\alpha\) test rejects \(H_0\) if \(A_c \ge z_\alpha \sigma\):\[T_\alpha = \mathbf{1}_{[z_\alpha \sigma, \infty)}(A_c)\]
  • The Finite-\(n\) Test: For \(n\) independent copies, let \(L = c_1 Q + c_2 P\). The statistic \(A_c\) corresponds to the normalized sum of local observables:\[A_c^{(n)} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \sum_{j=1}^n I^{\otimes (j-1)} \otimes L \otimes I^{\otimes (n-j)}\]The finite-sample test \(T_n = \mathbf{1}_{[z_\alpha \sigma, \infty)}(A_c^{(n)})\) achieves the asymptotic power bound.
